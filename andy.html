I begged to go to Canada instead of America because I heard they were more open and welcoming.

I knew Canada was multicultural and I imagined high school to have people of cultures laughing together while walking down the hallways. I wanted to meet people from all over the world and maybe get to visit their countries and sleep on their couches.

This wasn’t the reality, though. When I started high school, a month after moving here, I noticed that Canadian students never talked to me unless they needed to. We didn’t really have a presence to them, it seemed.

There was a divide between Canadian students and International students.

Halloween came around and so many Canadian students dressed up. We don’t celebrate Halloween in China and I was so confused when I saw all the costumes. It was awkward because none of my friends dressed up. I felt like we missed the memo.

By not dressing up for Halloween, we missed an opportunity for our classmates to accept us as part of the school.

With every school spirit event like Pajama Day, no one in my group really understood what school spirit was. We never had these events back home and no one told us why these events happened. That’s why we didn’t take part in these events.

I know everyone in the school is invited to these events, but I never felt invited because it was like we were never part of the school to begin with anyways.

One time, some Canadian students were selling lemonade in the hallways. They all all seemed confident and were talking and laughing with each other. But when my friends and I walked past, he never approached us or bothered to include us.

Maybe Canadian students didn’t bother to talk with us because they saw us as temporary since we were international students.

Andy's Experience

  • My dad sent me here to live by myself at 14 years old. I didn't want to leave my friends in Beijing.
  • I came to Canada because it is known for its multiculturalism and I believed it would be inclusive.
